Koho

May 2015 - September 2017

CTO

Koho Financial is a FinTech (Financial Technology) company, providing a modern mobile banking experience to Canadians.

I was the founding CTO at Koho.

I took the company from nothing, to having a working, functioning, scalable, and secure software technology system, to having a product launched in the market with customers.

I built out the company's technology, engineering, and product teams.

I helped scale the company.

At different times, what it meant to be CTO of Koho changed.

But it had always required a mix of skills; which includes: having a deep understanding of the technical, of the product, and of people.

I joined Koho a little before we received any funding. I was the original technical person, and built the initial technology.

When I was the only technical person at Koho, being CTO meant that I was a software engineer, an architect, a data scientist, a sysadmin, and a product developer.

After Koho got funding, it gave us the ability to recruit and hire our first team. This included both software engineers and product developers / managers.

When we started hiring our first team, being CTO meant I was a recruiter, a networker, and the public "face" of Koho to technical people.

This team continued the software and product development, and created the initial versions of Koho.

Once this first team was established, being CTO meant I was a Manager, a Lead, a mentor, a trainer, a Technical Director, and the Chief Architect.

I helped junior and intermediate software engineers turn into senior software engineers. And set senior software engineers on the path to becoming leads, managers, and principal software engineers.

I provided the technical vision.

I provided oversight for everyone's work.

Later, as the company grew and needed to scale, I created a number of new teams, each with their own mandates and responsibilities. And each with their own leads or managers.

Once multiple teams were created, being CTO meant I was a Director, I was a Lead of other Leads, and a Manager of other Managers.

Each time the company went through these qualitative changes, I hired others to do jobs I was previously doing myself.

At different times, what it meant to be CTO of Koho changed, but it had always required a mix of skills. At different times, being CTO meant being a software manager, being a technical director, being a software engineer, being a software architect, being a product manager, being a sysadmin (operations / DevOps), being a data scientist, being a mentor, being a recruiter, and being a lead.

I also helped Koho close their $42 million series B investment led by Power Corporation's Portag3 Ventures.
